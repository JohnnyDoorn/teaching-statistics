  • ownIQ: estimate of own IQ
  • nextIQ: estimate of neighbor's IQ
  • diffScores: difference scores computed as (ownIQ - nextIQ)
  • classPosition: this was randomly generated in order to demonstrate the independent samples t-test later

One of the assumptions of the t-test is that the data follow a normal distribution. This assumption is needed because that ensures that the sampling distribution we use for our inference is in fact the t-distribution (which forms the whole basis of our inferential framework). In most software, the option to test this assumption comes in the form of a hypothesis test, where a significant p-value indicates a violation of the assumption. Here, the "Shapiro-Wilk" test tests the normality assumption. We can see that for some variables, the normality assumption is violated. This could be grounds to instead opt for the non-parametric test (e.g., Wilcoxon rank-sum, or signed-rank tests), which are slightly less powerful, but are not based on the normality assumption (more on this in lecture 17).

For now, for demonstrative purposes, let's continue with each of the t-tests that we can conduct for this data, using an alpha of 0.05.

We start with a one sample t-test to test whether people's estimates of their own IQ differs from the average IQ of bachelor students in the Netherlands (120). To do so, "ownIQ" was dragged into the Variables box on the lefthand side, and then "Test value" was set to 120. This conducts a significance test to see if the average of ownIQ differs significantly from 120 (supposedly the average IQ of a college student).

The t-statistic and corresponding p-value seem to be significant for the specified alpha level, which means we reject the null hypothesis. The effect size suggests that there is a fairly large difference between 120 and the estimates of one's own IQ. Specifically, people seemed to have been underestimating their IQ. Perhaps because of modesty? Or because people are very focused on the average IQ of 100 in the general population? Or maybe people are perfect estimators of their own IQ's and this claimed 120 IQ for college students is simply bogus?

Below the observed average IQ is displayed, including a 95% confidence interval. As you can see, 120 falls outside of the interval, which is another indication that the estimated IQ deviates from 120. From this graph (or the CI in the table above), we can see that the underestimation of IQ ranges between 4 and 12 IQ points, which is a pretty big gap in terms of IQ points.

Next, we can test whether there is a difference between estimates of one's own IQ and estimates of their neighbors' IQ scores. To do so, conduct a paired samples t-test, because the observations are made "within subject", meaning that each observation for "ownIQ" has an observation for "nextIQ" that is made by the same participant. We can use the following two hypotheses:

Based on the paired samples t-test and prespecified alpha of 0.05, we cannot reject the null hypothesis. This does not mean that we found evidence for there being no difference (i.e., evidence of absence), but it only means that we do not have evidence for there being a difference (i.e., absence of evidence).
