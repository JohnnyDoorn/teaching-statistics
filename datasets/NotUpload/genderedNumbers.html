Below is the analysis of the asscociation between two categorical variables:

  • Type of number: odd or even (independent variable)
  • Association: feminine or masculine (dependent variable)

To assess a categorical association, we can use the X2 statistic to quantify the difference between the observed data (observed frequencies in each cell in the table below) and the expected data under H0.

H0 postulates that the two variables are independent. This has the following implication: if Type and Association are independent, the probability of, for instance, observing (Association = Feminine AND Type = Even), is equal to the probability P(Association = Feminine) * P(Type = Even). Using this multiplication rule, we can compute the expected cell frequencies based on the assumption that Type and Association are independent. For (Association = Feminine AND Type = Even) this computation is as follows: Frequency of Feminine * Frequency of Even / n = (139 * 126) / 252 = 69.5.

The X2 statistic then sums up all the squared differences between observed and expected. For these data, the X2 equals 35.441. To get the association p-value, we then locate the observed value in the sampling distribution under H0 (the X2 distribution with 1 df), to get the probability of observing 35.441, or more extreme, if the null hypothesis were true. In this case, the p-value is very low (p < 0.001). This suggests the presence of an association between the type of number, and whether it has a masculine or feminine vibe.

There are different test statistics we could look at in the X2 test. There are the X2 and the corrected X2 (in case of small cell sizes), but also the likelihood ratio. While the former two statistics are based on the squared differences between observed and expected cell frequencies, the likelihood ratio is based on the ratio between observed and expected frequencies. Both statistics quantify the discrepancy between observed and expected, and will generally agree (if they yield qualitatively different conclusions, this warrants further investigation, for instance there might an issue with the sample size that was used).

In addition to looking at the X2 statistic (i.e., the test statistic), we can also look at the effect size, to quantify the magnitude of the observed effect. The odds (computed by hand) are:

  • For the even numbers, there are 96/33 = 2.9 times as many feminine associations than masculine associations.
  • For the odd numbers, there are 46/83 = 0.55 times as many feminine associations than masculine associations.

If we take these together, we can quantify how many more likely it is to receive a feminine association for an even number compared to an odd number, by taking the ratio of these two values: 2.9 / 0.55 = 5.25.

We can also do this the other way around: it is about 0.55 / 2.9 = 0.25 as likely for an odd number to receive a feminine association, compared to an even number.

In doing so, we control for people's tendency to respond "feminine" to any number: if we would have found more people responding "feminine" for even numbers (odds for even numbers; 2.9), while those odds are the same for odd numbers, there is simply a bias of people to respond "feminine" to any number. If the two odds are the same, the odds ratio equals 1, and so there would be no effect whatsoever. So by looking at the odds ratio, we take into account the base rate of "feminine" responses. As with any effect size that is a ratio, 1 means there is no effect, and deviations from 1 indicate an effect.

So what do we conclude based on this data? Well, we found quite some evidence for an association between Type and Association (X2 = 35.4, p < 0.001). Based on the odds ratio, we can conclude that an even number is about 5.25 as likely to have a "feminine" association as an odd number. Conversely, odd numbers are about 5.25 more likely to have a "masculine" association than even numbers.

This seems to corroborate the original study by Wilkie and Bodenhausen: "We confirmed that odd numbers seemed masculine while even numbers seemed feminine."
